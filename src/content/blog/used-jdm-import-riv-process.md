Canada is one of the most permissive [JDM](/glossary/jdm/) import markets in North America. The federal threshold for importing a non-Canadian-spec vehicle is fifteen years from date of manufacture — meaning in 2026, any JDM vehicle built in 2011 or earlier can be legally imported, modified for the relaxed Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards that apply to vehicles 15+ years old, and registered for road use in any Canadian province. The United States, by comparison, requires twenty-five years.

The federal process is administered through the [Registrar of Imported Vehicles](/glossary/riv-program/) — abbreviated RIV — under contract to Transport Canada. This piece walks through the complete federal RIV process step by step, what each step costs, what timeline to expect, and where the most common buyer mistakes happen.

## What is the 15-year rule?

The 15-year rule is the federal exemption threshold above which a non-Canadian-spec vehicle can be imported into Canada and registered for road use without meeting current Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards. It is set out in the Motor Vehicle Safety Act and administered through the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/).

Below 15 years from date of manufacture, only vehicles that meet CMVSS at the time of manufacture can be imported. In practice this means only vehicles built for the Canadian or US market — vehicles whose original-equipment specifications happen to align with North American safety regulations. JDM vehicles built for the Japanese domestic market are generally not in this category and cannot be imported below the 15-year threshold.

Above 15 years from date of manufacture, the safety-modification requirements relax substantially. Many CMVSS items — current crash-test compliance, current emissions standards, current daytime running lights specifications — do not apply to vehicles 15+ years old. The required modifications are typically minimal, and the federal RIV inspection focuses on verifying that any required modifications were completed correctly and that the vehicle is generally roadworthy.

The age is calculated from the original date of manufacture, not the model year. A JDM vehicle stamped with a January 2011 production date can be imported in January 2026. A vehicle stamped with a December 2011 production date must wait until December 2026. Verify the production date — usually visible on the door-jamb sticker or the firewall plate — before purchase.

## Step 1: Customs entry at the port of arrival

The federal process starts at the Canada Border Services Agency. When the vehicle arrives at the Canadian port of entry — typically the Port of Vancouver for Pacific arrivals or the Port of Halifax/Toronto for Atlantic and overland arrivals — the importer or their broker presents Form 1 (the Vehicle Import Form) to CBSA along with the original Japanese export certificate, bill of lading, and proof of purchase from the foreign seller.

CBSA collects applicable import duties (typically duty-free for vehicles under CUSMA, but duty applies on certain non-NAFTA-origin shipments), GST (5% federal), and any provincial sales tax that applies at import. The provincial tax structure varies: BC charges PST at import, Ontario charges HST equivalent, Alberta charges only GST, Quebec applies QST.

The total CBSA cost on a JDM vehicle is calculated on the landed value — the auction or purchase price plus shipping plus marine insurance — at the federal duty rate (often 0% under CUSMA), federal GST (5%), and provincial sales tax depending on the destination province. For a vehicle with a $40,000 landed value being imported to Ontario, expect approximately $5,200 in HST plus minimal or no duty.

CBSA processes Form 1 typically same-day at the port of entry if all documentation is in order. The vehicle is released to the importer (or import broker) with the Form 1 stamped, allowing the importer to proceed to the RIV process and avoid demurrage and storage charges that accrue if the vehicle remains at the port.

## Step 2: RIV programme fee and modifications

After CBSA release, the importer pays the RIV programme fee through the official RIV portal at riv.ca. The fee is currently $325 plus applicable provincial sales tax — verify the current fee at the time of import as it has changed periodically over the programme's history. The fee covers the inspection process, modifications verification, and recall clearance.

Required modifications depend on the specific vehicle and its age. The most common modifications for JDM vehicles 15+ years old are: daytime running lights (typically $200 to $500 installed at any auto electrical shop), occasionally child-tether anchors on certain vehicles (typically $100 to $300), and rarely metric instrument cluster work on pre-2000 imports where Japanese-market specifications materially diverge from current Canadian requirements.

Manufacturer recall clearance is verified by the vehicle's [VIN](/glossary/vin/) through the Canadian dealer network. Open recalls applicable to the imported vehicle must be cleared at any same-brand Canadian dealer at no charge — manufacturers are legally obligated under the Motor Vehicle Safety Act to repair safety recalls regardless of vehicle age or current ownership. The recall verification typically takes one to three business days at a dealer parts/service department.

## Step 3: Federal Form 2 inspection at Canadian Tire

The federal RIV inspection is performed at a designated inspection facility — and the dominant designated network is Canadian Tire. The full list of designated facilities is maintained at riv.ca and covers Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver, Calgary, Edmonton, Ottawa, and most secondary Canadian cities.

The inspection itself takes 30 to 60 minutes if no issues are found. The inspector verifies that any required modifications were completed correctly, that recalls have been cleared, and that the vehicle generally meets the relaxed CMVSS requirements applicable to its age. Common failure points are incomplete daytime running lights installation, unresolved recalls, and rare cases where the vehicle's specifications diverge from acceptable Canadian standards in ways that require additional modification.

If the vehicle fails inspection, the inspector issues a list of required corrections. The importer addresses the corrections (typically through a return visit to the auto electrical shop or dealer for additional work) and presents the vehicle for re-inspection. Most JDM imports clear inspection on the first or second attempt.

Booking lead times for RIV inspection at Canadian Tire run one to three weeks during peak import seasons (spring and summer), particularly in Vancouver where most JDM imports arrive at the Port of Vancouver. Importers should book the RIV inspection appointment as soon as customs clearance is complete — calling the Canadian Tire RIV inspection line directly is typically the fastest path to a confirmed slot.

## Step 4: Provincial registration and safety inspection

Federal RIV completion is necessary but not sufficient for road use. Each province has its own registration and safety inspection requirements that apply on top of the federal process. The [Provincial Safety Inspection](/glossary/provincial-safety-inspection/) verifies mechanical roadworthiness — brakes, suspension, steering, lights, tires, exhaust, structural condition — and is separate from the federal RIV inspection.

Ontario requires a Safety Standards Certificate (SSC), valid for 36 days from issue, performed at a licensed Motor Vehicle Inspection Station. Cost typically runs $80 to $150 plus any required repairs. Alberta requires an Out-of-Province Inspection (OOPI) at an [AMVIC](/glossary/amvic/)-licensed facility, typically $150 to $250 plus repairs. British Columbia requires Designated Inspection Facility inspection for most JDM imports, typically $250 to $500 plus repairs. Quebec requires SAAQ mechanical inspection. Other provinces apply variations of these models.

Once the provincial safety is complete, the importer registers the vehicle through the provincial registration office (ServiceOntario in Ontario, Service Alberta in Alberta, ICBC in BC, SAAQ in Quebec). Registration fees run $70 to $150 depending on province, plus plates, plus any titling and document fees. Insurance arrangements are completed at the same time — confirm coverage availability before finalizing import on right-hand-drive vehicles or specialty JDM models, as some Canadian insurers decline coverage on certain RHD or specialty imports.

## Total cost summary

For a typical mid-value JDM import — say a $25,000 CAD landed-value vehicle being imported to Ontario — total federal process cost runs approximately:

- CBSA duties: $0 (typically duty-free under CUSMA)
- Federal GST: $1,250 (5% of $25,000)
- Ontario HST: $3,250 (13% of $25,000)  
- RIV programme fee: $325 plus HST = $367
- Required modifications (daytime running lights typical): $200 to $500
- Recall clearance (no charge from dealer)
- Federal RIV inspection: included in fee
- Provincial Safety Standards Certificate: $80 to $150 plus any required repairs
- Provincial registration and plates: $90 to $150
- Insurance setup: varies by carrier

Total federal-and-provincial process cost on top of the $25,000 landed-value vehicle: approximately $5,500 to $6,200, of which roughly $4,500 is taxes (HST plus GST). The non-tax federal-and-provincial process cost is approximately $1,000 to $1,700.

## Common buyer mistakes

Three mistakes recur in first-time JDM imports. First, underestimating the timeline — buyers expecting "a few weeks" from auction to plates are typically surprised by 8 to 12 weeks total, with peak-season delays adding another 2 to 4 weeks. Build the timeline into purchase expectations.

Second, skipping the auction grade verification. Japanese auction houses use a grading system that captures pre-import damage history. A "clean" auction-bought JDM may carry a grade of 4 or 4.5 (minor cosmetic flaws) or it may carry a grade of R, RA, or A (accident damage with varying severity of repair). The grade does not trigger Canadian [Salvage Title](/glossary/salvage-title/) branding because the damage occurred in Japan rather than through Canadian insurance-total claim. Pre-purchase inspection plus paint-thickness gauge check on body panels is non-negotiable on any JDM import.

Third, not confirming insurance availability before importing. Some Canadian insurers decline right-hand-drive vehicles entirely. Some decline specialty JDM models (Skyline GT-R, certain kei vehicles). Some impose surcharges that meaningfully change the total ownership cost. Confirm insurance coverage and pricing with your specific insurer before committing to import.

## Common questions

### What is the 15-year rule for importing cars to Canada?

The 15-year rule is the federal threshold above which a non-Canadian-spec vehicle can be imported into Canada under the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) and registered for road use without meeting current Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards. The age is calculated from the original date of manufacture (not the model year). Vehicles below 15 years can only be imported if they already meet CMVSS at time of manufacture — typically only Canadian-market or US-market vehicles. The 15-year threshold is the structural reason Canada is a permissive [JDM](/glossary/jdm/) market compared to the US 25-year rule.

### How much does the RIV process cost?

The RIV programme fee is currently $325 CAD plus applicable provincial sales tax (HST in Ontario, GST plus PST in BC, GST only in Alberta) — verify the current fee at riv.ca at the time of import. Required modifications typically add $200 to $800 (daytime running lights are the most common). Federal inspection at Canadian Tire is included in the fee. Provincial safety inspection adds another $80 to $500 depending on province plus any required repairs. Total non-tax federal-and-provincial process cost runs approximately $1,000 to $1,700 for typical JDM imports. The RIV process is separate from the larger CBSA duty, GST, and provincial sales tax that apply at import.

### Where do I do the RIV inspection?

At a designated Canadian Tire facility — the largest network of RIV-designated inspection facilities in Canada. Designated facilities operate in Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver, Calgary, Edmonton, Ottawa, and most secondary Canadian cities. The full list is maintained at riv.ca. The inspection itself takes 30 to 60 minutes if no issues are found. Booking lead times run one to three weeks during peak import seasons. Call the Canadian Tire RIV inspection line directly to schedule the appointment as soon as your customs clearance and required modifications are complete.

### What modifications does Canada require on JDM imports?

For JDM vehicles 15+ years old, the modification requirements are typically minimal. The most common requirement is daytime running lights — a Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standard that requires headlights or DRLs to be active during daylight driving. Installation typically runs $200 to $500 at any auto electrical shop. Occasionally child-tether anchors are required on certain vehicles ($100 to $300). Rarely, metric instrument cluster work is needed on pre-2000 imports. The required modifications are listed on the RIV form for each specific vehicle and verified by the federal inspector at the Canadian Tire RIV inspection.

### How long does customs clearance take?

CBSA processes Form 1 typically same-day at the port of entry if all documentation is in order — the vehicle's original Japanese export certificate, bill of lading, and proof of purchase. Import brokers handle this routine. The full federal RIV process — from CBSA release to RIV inspection completion — typically takes 4 to 8 weeks. Adding provincial safety inspection and registration brings the total to 6 to 12 weeks from arrival at the Canadian port to plates on the vehicle. Peak seasons (spring and summer) extend timelines by 2 to 4 weeks, particularly at the Port of Vancouver.
