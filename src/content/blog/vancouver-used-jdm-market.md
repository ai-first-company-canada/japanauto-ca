Vancouver moves more JDM steel than any other Canadian city. Roberts Bank and Vanterm container terminals handle most Pacific arrivals, the import broker community in Richmond and Burnaby has been processing 15-year imports for two decades, and the BC enthusiast and small-business demand for kei trucks, performance imports, and specialty Japanese vehicles is structurally larger than in any other Canadian market. If you are buying [JDM](/glossary/jdm/) used in 2026 — whether as a daily driver, weekend project, work vehicle, or investment — Vancouver is where the supply lives.

This piece walks through how the import process actually works, what auction grades to look for, what the total landed cost looks like, and the BC-specific registration and insurance considerations that affect daily-driver use.

## How does the JDM import process work in 2026?

The federal threshold is fifteen years from date of manufacture. As of 2026, that means any JDM vehicle built in 2011 or earlier can legally be imported, modified for relaxed Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards where required, and registered for road use in BC. The process has several steps and typically takes 6 to 12 weeks from purchase at a Japanese auction to BC plates on the vehicle.

First, the purchase itself. Most JDM imports are bought at Japanese auto auctions — USS, JU, TAA are the major auction networks. A Vancouver-based import broker bids on the buyer's behalf, wins the auction, arranges shipping by roll-on roll-off (RoRo) ship from Yokohama or Nagoya, and handles documentation through customs clearance. Brokers typically charge $1,500 to $3,500 CAD service fees on top of the auction price, shipping costs, and CBSA duties.

Second, customs and the federal [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/). On arrival at Vancouver, the broker completes Form 1 with the Canada Border Services Agency, pays applicable duties (typically duty-free for vehicles under CUSMA but GST and BC PST apply at import), pays the RIV fee ($325 plus tax in BC), completes any required modifications (daytime running lights are the most common), clears outstanding manufacturer recalls verified by [VIN](/glossary/vin/), and presents the vehicle at a designated RIV inspection facility — typically a Canadian Tire — for the federal Form 2 inspection.

Third, BC registration. Once the RIV process is complete, the vehicle goes through BC safety inspection if required (most Vancouver-area registrations require Designated Inspection Facility inspection), receives provincial plates and registration through ICBC, and the buyer obtains insurance coverage.

## What is the total cost of importing a JDM vehicle?

The math is more complex than the auction price suggests. A typical JDM Honda Acty kei truck purchased at auction in Japan for ¥350,000 (roughly CAD $3,200) lands in Vancouver at approximately CAD $7,500 to $9,500 all-in including shipping, broker fees, customs duties, GST, BC PST, RIV fee, modifications, and inspection. The "all-in landed cost" runs roughly 25 to 35 percent above the auction hammer price plus shipping for typical kei trucks and small JDM vehicles.

For higher-value JDM imports — Nissan Skyline GT-R R32/R33/R34, Toyota Chaser JZX100, Honda S660 sports kei — the percentage uplift is similar but the absolute dollars are larger. A clean R32 GT-R purchased at auction for ¥5 million (roughly CAD $46,000) typically lands in Vancouver at CAD $58,000 to $66,000 all-in. The same R32 GT-R relisted at a Vancouver retail dealer typically runs CAD $70,000 to $90,000 — the dealer markup on the import-process margin.

Buyer-side cost categories that add up:
- Auction hammer price (the headline number)
- Japanese auction broker commission (typically ¥30,000 to ¥80,000)
- Domestic transport in Japan to the export port
- RoRo shipping (typically CAD $1,800 to $3,500 for a passenger vehicle)
- Marine insurance during transit
- CBSA duties and import tax
- GST on landed value (5% federal)
- BC PST on landed value (12% on most vehicles, scaling higher above $125,000)
- RIV fee ($325 plus BC tax)
- Required modifications (typically $200 to $800 for daytime running lights)
- Recall clearance and federal RIV inspection
- BC safety inspection if required ($250 to $500)
- BC registration and ICBC insurance

For first-time JDM buyers, working with a broker who handles the full chain is usually the right approach. Self-importing is possible but adds complexity and risk in exchange for typically $2,000 to $4,000 in cost savings.

## What auction grade should I look for?

Japanese auction houses use a grading system that matters more than most North American buyers realize. The grades range from "S" (effectively new, often demonstration vehicles) down through "6, 5, 4.5, 4, 3.5, 3, 2, 1, R, RA, A," with each grade reflecting specific condition, mileage, and damage history.

For a Vancouver buyer who wants a clean daily-driver JDM, target grade 4 or higher. A grade 4 vehicle has minor cosmetic flaws but no mechanical issues or significant damage history. Grade 4.5 and 5 are progressively cleaner. Grade 3 and below typically indicates documented damage repair, mileage concerns, or mechanical issues — these vehicles can be value plays for project car buyers but are not appropriate for first-time JDM purchases.

The "R" grade indicates accident damage that has been repaired. "RA" indicates damage to multiple body panels with repair history. "A" indicates accident damage that has not been repaired. None of these grades trigger automatic Canadian [Salvage Title](/glossary/salvage-title/) branding because the damage occurred in Japan rather than through a Canadian insurance-total claim — which is why pre-purchase inspection plus paint-thickness gauge check on body panels is non-negotiable on any JDM import regardless of CarFax-clean status.

Auction sheets contain detailed condition diagrams marking specific panel damage, paint repair, frame condition, and interior wear. A reputable Vancouver import broker will provide the auction sheet and an English translation before purchase. Walk away from any broker who cannot or will not.

## Are right-hand-drive cars legal in BC?

Yes, with caveats. Right-hand-drive vehicles imported under the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) 15-year rule are legal for road registration in British Columbia. The Vehicle Sales Authority of British Columbia ([VSA](/glossary/vsa/)) does not impose RHD-specific restrictions beyond the federal RIV process, and ICBC will write registration on RHD vehicles without RHD-specific surcharges in most cases.

The practical constraints are insurance and daily-driver use. ICBC's basic Autoplan coverage is available on RHD vehicles, but optional collision coverage may be quoted at higher rates than equivalent left-hand-drive vehicles. Some optional-coverage providers operating in BC decline RHD coverage entirely. Confirm coverage with your insurer before importing.

For daily-driver use specifically, RHD ergonomics affect routine driving in measurable ways. Highway tollbooths, parkade tickets, and drive-through windows are all designed for left-hand-drive vehicles. Passing slower traffic on undivided roads requires more careful judgment because the driver sits on the wrong side of the road for forward visibility around the vehicle ahead. Many Vancouver JDM buyers use RHD vehicles for weekend driving and project car use rather than daily commuting for these reasons.

## Daily-driver vs project car: how to think about JDM ownership

Most first-time JDM buyers underestimate the difference between owning a JDM as a daily driver versus as a weekend project. The daily-driver case applies to imports where the underlying vehicle was sold globally — Japanese-spec versions of the Toyota Corolla, Honda Civic, Nissan Pathfinder. The project-car case applies to JDM-only models like the Toyota Chaser, Honda Acty, Nissan Skyline GT-R, and similar specialty vehicles where parts availability, mechanical service network, and Canadian insurance pricing all reflect the niche-import status.

Daily-driver JDM in Vancouver works for buyers who: are comfortable with right-hand-drive ergonomics, have a backup vehicle for inclement weather, have a relationship with an independent shop that handles JDM service (Vancouver has several), and are willing to source some parts through Japanese specialty importers (Amayama, Megazip) rather than through a Canadian dealer. For buyers who do not match those criteria, a Canadian-spec used Japanese vehicle is typically the better daily-driver choice.

Project-car JDM works in a different mode. The vehicle sees occasional weekend use, custom maintenance and parts sourcing are part of the appeal, and the Canadian-market resale community for clean JDM specialty cars is mature enough that depreciation can flatten or reverse on the right examples held over multi-year horizons. Vancouver's JDM enthusiast community is the largest in Canada and supports both casual and serious project-car ownership.

## Common questions

### How long does it take to import a JDM car to Vancouver?

Typical timeline from auction purchase to BC plates is 6 to 12 weeks. The breakdown: 1 to 3 weeks for Japanese auction processing and domestic transport to export port, 3 to 5 weeks for RoRo shipping across the Pacific, 1 to 3 weeks for CBSA customs clearance and federal [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) inspection at Canadian Tire, 1 to 2 weeks for required modifications and recall clearance, and 1 week for BC safety inspection (if required) and ICBC registration. Peak season (spring and summer) extends timelines because RIV inspection facilities run booked. For time-sensitive buyers, factor in 12 to 16 weeks rather than the optimistic 6.

### What is the total cost of importing a JDM vehicle?

Total landed cost in Vancouver typically runs 25 to 35 percent above the auction hammer price plus shipping. For a kei truck purchased at auction for CAD $3,500 equivalent, expect $7,500 to $9,500 all-in. For a clean R32 GT-R at CAD $46,000 auction equivalent, expect $58,000 to $66,000 all-in. Cost categories include broker commission, RoRo shipping, marine insurance, CBSA duties, GST plus BC PST, RIV fee ($325), required modifications, federal and provincial inspections, and ICBC registration. First-time importers should budget the higher end of the range; experienced importers working with established brokers can sometimes achieve the lower end.

### Are right-hand-drive cars legal in BC?

Yes. Right-hand-drive vehicles imported under the federal [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) 15-year rule are legal for BC road registration, and the [VSA](/glossary/vsa/) does not impose RHD-specific restrictions beyond the federal process. ICBC writes basic Autoplan coverage on RHD vehicles. Optional collision and comprehensive coverage may be available at standard or modestly elevated rates depending on insurer. Some optional-coverage providers decline RHD coverage entirely. Verify insurance availability before importing.

### What auction grade should I look for on a JDM import?

For a clean daily-driver JDM, target grade 4 or higher on Japanese auction grading. Grade 4 indicates minor cosmetic flaws but no mechanical issues or significant damage. Grades 4.5, 5, 6, and S are progressively cleaner. Grade 3 and below typically indicates documented repair history, mileage concerns, or mechanical issues — appropriate for project-car buyers but not for first-time JDM purchases. The "R" grade indicates repaired accident damage; "RA" indicates multi-panel repair; "A" indicates unrepaired accident damage. None of these auction grades trigger automatic Canadian [Salvage Title](/glossary/salvage-title/) branding, which is why pre-purchase inspection plus paint-thickness gauge check on body panels is non-negotiable on any JDM import.

### Can I drive a JDM car as a daily driver in Vancouver?

For Japanese-spec versions of globally-sold vehicles (JDM Civic, JDM Corolla, JDM Pathfinder), yes — these work as daily drivers with proper service support. For specialty JDM-only models (Skyline GT-R, Chaser, Acty kei truck, S660 sports kei), daily-driver use is feasible but adds complexity: right-hand-drive ergonomics, parts sourcing through Japanese specialty importers, an independent shop relationship for service, and insurance considerations. Vancouver's JDM ecosystem supports daily-driver ownership better than most Canadian cities, but the trade-offs are real. Project-car or weekend-driver use is typically the more common ownership pattern.
