## What is JDM?

JDM stands for Japanese Domestic Market. The term describes vehicles, engines, transmissions, and parts that were originally manufactured for retail sale inside Japan rather than for export. A JDM-spec Honda Civic Type R built for Tokyo showrooms in 2007 is a different vehicle from the export-spec Civic Si sold in Calgary the same year — different chassis tuning, different emissions equipment, different interior trim, and right-hand drive.

The category covers more than the famous performance cars. Toyota Chaser, Nissan Skyline GT-R R32 through R34, Mitsubishi Lancer Evolution, Subaru Impreza WRX STi 22B — those are the headline JDM imports. But the category also includes the more mundane: Honda Acty kei trucks, Nissan Pulsar GTI-R, Toyota Hiace vans in configurations the export market never saw, and the right-hand-drive variants of mass-market sedans that some Canadian buyers prefer for novelty rather than performance.

Many JDM vehicles also carry Japanese-market emissions and inspection markers, including the Shaken inspection sticker and original Japanese registration plates. Those are not legal documents in Canada and are typically removed during the RIV process, but they tell you the vehicle's documented history before import.

## Why it matters in Canada

Canada is one of the most permissive JDM import markets in North America, second only to certain US territories. The federal threshold under the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) is fifteen years from date of manufacture — meaning in 2026, any JDM vehicle built in 2011 or earlier can legally be imported, modified for Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards where required, and registered for road use. The United States, by contrast, requires twenty-five years.

Vancouver and Toronto are the two dominant JDM import hubs in the country. The Port of Vancouver handles most Pacific arrivals — vehicles loaded onto roll-on roll-off ships in Yokohama or Nagoya land at Roberts Bank or the Vanterm container terminal — and from there they move into BC dealer networks or onto trucks for buyers across Western Canada. Toronto absorbs the Eastern Canadian volume through customs at Pearson and the port terminals on Lake Ontario.

A JDM vehicle imported to Canada needs Form 1 from the Canada Border Services Agency, payment of the RIV fee plus duties, completion of any required modifications (daytime running lights are the most common), recall clearance, and the federal Form 2 inspection at a designated facility — typically a Canadian Tire. Then the provincial safety inspection comes on top. In Alberta that's the Out-of-Province Inspection at an [AMVIC](/glossary/amvic/)-licensed shop. In Ontario, a Safety Standards Certificate.

## Common questions

### Can I import a JDM car to Canada?

Yes, provided the vehicle is at least fifteen years old as of the import date. The age is calculated from date of manufacture, not model year, and the threshold is enforced by Transport Canada at the port of entry. Vehicles younger than fifteen years cannot be imported as JDM unless they meet a narrow set of exemptions (matching Canadian-market specification, manufacturer-authorized import, certain commercial or research use). The most common path is for a buyer or broker to purchase from a Japanese auction (USS, JU, TAA), arrange shipping by RoRo or container, clear customs in Vancouver or Toronto, and complete the RIV process before provincial registration.

### What is the 15-year rule for importing cars to Canada?

The 15-year rule is the federal threshold above which a non-Canadian-spec vehicle can be imported into Canada and registered for road use without meeting current Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards. It is set out in the Motor Vehicle Safety Act and administered through the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/). Below fifteen years, only vehicles that meet CMVSS at time of manufacture — meaning, in practice, only vehicles built for the Canadian or US market — can be imported. Above fifteen years, the safety-modification requirements relax substantially because the vehicle is treated as a classic-market import.

### What is the difference between a JDM and a Canadian-spec car?

A Canadian-spec car was built for sale in Canada, meets all CMVSS at time of manufacture, and was likely sold new through a Canadian dealer network. A JDM car was built for Japan, often features right-hand drive, may use Japan-specific emissions hardware, often comes from the factory with kilometre-denominated odometers (now standard worldwide but earlier mile/km swap is sometimes seen), and may carry safety equipment that does not meet current Canadian standards. Mechanical reliability is typically comparable or better — JDM vehicles often have lower lifetime kilometres and stricter emissions inspection — but documentation and parts cross-reference can be more complex.

### Are JDM cars legal in all Canadian provinces?

Federally legal once they pass the [RIV](/glossary/riv-program/) process, but provincial safety inspection rules differ. British Columbia and Quebec are generally the most accommodating — Vancouver has a mature JDM ecosystem with shops that specialize in the inspection paperwork. Ontario has historically been stricter on right-hand-drive imports, particularly for daily-driver registration, and some insurance carriers will charge a surcharge or decline coverage on RHD vehicles regardless of province. Confirm both the provincial safety requirements and your insurer's RHD policy before purchasing a JDM import.
