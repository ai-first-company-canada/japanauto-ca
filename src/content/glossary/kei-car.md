## What is a kei car?

A kei car — formally 軽自動車 (kei-jidousha, "light automobile") — is a class of Japanese vehicle defined by Japanese tax and registration regulations rather than by performance or styling. The current Japanese rules limit kei vehicles to maximum dimensions of 3.4 m length, 1.48 m width, 2.0 m height, and 660 cc engine displacement. Vehicles meeting those constraints qualify for reduced Japanese road tax, parking exemptions in some Japanese cities, and the distinctive yellow licence plate that distinguishes them from regular passenger vehicles.

The category was originally created in 1949 to accelerate post-war Japanese motorisation by making small, cheap vehicles affordable for working-class families. The original limits were tighter — 150 cc engines and shorter dimensions — and have been progressively relaxed over the decades. The current 660 cc / 3.4-metre standard has been in place since 1990. Kei vehicles cover a remarkable range of body styles: small passenger cars (Honda N-Box, Suzuki Wagon R, Daihatsu Move), kei trucks (Honda Acty, Suzuki Carry, Daihatsu Hijet), kei vans, and even sports cars (Honda Beat, Honda S660, Mazda Autozam AZ-1).

Within the [Japanese-brand](/glossary/jdm/) whitelist on japanauto.ca — Toyota, Honda, Nissan, Mazda, Subaru, Lexus, Acura, Infiniti, Mitsubishi — the kei vehicles relevant to Canadian buyers are primarily Honda kei trucks (the Acty, discontinued in 2021 but available as a 15-year [RIV](/glossary/riv-program/) import) and Honda's sports kei vehicles (the Beat from the 1990s and the S660 from 2015 onward, both importable under the 15-year rule).

## Why it matters in Canada

Kei vehicles are importable to Canada under the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/) 15-year rule. Once a kei truck or sports kei reaches fifteen years from date of manufacture, it can be imported, modified for Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards where required (typically minimal modifications because of the relaxed CMVSS exemptions for vehicles 15+ years old), cleared through customs at Vancouver or Toronto, and registered for road use.

The provincial registration outcome varies. British Columbia and Quebec are generally accommodating — Vancouver in particular has a mature kei truck import network serving farms, ski resorts, golf courses, and small businesses where the kei truck's compact dimensions and very low purchase cost (often under $10,000 CAD landed) make commercial sense. Alberta similarly accepts kei trucks for many use cases. Ontario has historically been more restrictive on right-hand-drive vehicle registration generally, and kei vehicles fall into the broader RHD-import questions that Ontario insurance carriers and the Ministry of Transportation evaluate case-by-case.

Insurance is the practical constraint that matters more than registration. Even in provinces where kei vehicles are road-legal, some insurance carriers decline coverage on kei trucks for daily-driver use or charge surcharges that erode the cost advantage. Confirm both registration eligibility and insurance availability before importing.

## Common questions

### Can I drive a kei truck in Canada?

In most Canadian provinces, yes — particularly British Columbia, Quebec, and Alberta where kei trucks have a mature import community. Once the vehicle is 15+ years old, imported through the [RIV Program](/glossary/riv-program/), and provincially registered with appropriate safety inspection, it is legal for road use. Insurance is the practical constraint that varies by carrier — confirm coverage availability before purchase. Ontario has historically been more restrictive on right-hand-drive imports including kei vehicles, with case-by-case Ministry of Transportation evaluation. Off-highway use (farms, golf courses, private property) is broadly accepted across all provinces.

### What does kei mean in Japanese?

Kei (軽) literally means "light" or "lightweight" in Japanese. The full term kei-jidousha (軽自動車) translates to "light automobile" and refers to the Japanese tax and registration category for small vehicles meeting specific dimension and engine-displacement limits. The "lightweight" designation refers to weight class for taxation and registration purposes rather than physical lightness — though kei vehicles are necessarily small and relatively light because of the dimensional limits.

### Is a kei car the same as a JDM car?

Kei is a subcategory within [JDM](/glossary/jdm/). All kei vehicles are JDM by definition because they are built for sale within Japan under Japanese-specific tax and registration rules — they would not exist as a category outside the Japanese regulatory framework. Not all JDM vehicles are kei: the Honda Civic Type R, Nissan Skyline GT-R, Toyota Chaser, and most Japan-domestic-market mainstream sedans and crossovers are JDM but not kei because they exceed the kei dimension and displacement limits. JDM is the broader category; kei is the specific small-vehicle subset.

### Are kei trucks legal in Ontario?

Ontario has historically been more restrictive on right-hand-drive vehicle imports than British Columbia or Quebec, including kei trucks. The Ministry of Transportation evaluates RHD vehicles case-by-case for road registration, and some insurance carriers decline coverage on RHD vehicles for daily-driver use in Ontario regardless of provincial registration eligibility. Off-highway use on private property — farms, golf courses, ski resorts — is broadly accepted across Ontario without registration constraints. For prospective Ontario buyers of a kei truck for road use, verify both Ministry of Transportation registration eligibility and insurance availability before importing.
