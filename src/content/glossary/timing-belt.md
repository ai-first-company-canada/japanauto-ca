## What is a timing belt?

A timing belt is a toothed reinforced rubber belt that synchronises the rotation of an engine's crankshaft with its camshafts. The synchronization is critical because the camshafts control when the intake and exhaust valves open and close relative to the position of each piston. If the timing slips even a few teeth, the valves and pistons move out of phase — and on most modern Japanese engines, that means the open valves and the rising piston meet at a force that bends the valves and damages the cylinder head.

Engines that suffer this kind of damage on timing failure are called "interference engines." Most modern Japanese engines designed for fuel-economy efficiency are interference designs, including the Honda J-series V6 (J35) fitted to the Odyssey, Pilot, and Acura MDX, the older Toyota 2GR-FE V6, and many older four-cylinder Toyotas and Hondas. The trade-off is that interference designs allow tighter valve clearances and higher compression ratios, which improves efficiency, at the cost of catastrophic damage if the timing component fails.

Many Japanese manufacturers transitioned from timing belts to timing chains during the 2000s. Modern Toyota A25A four-cylinder, Honda K20C, the 1.5L L15 turbo, and most Mazda [SkyActiv](/glossary/skyactiv/) engines now use timing chains, which last the operational life of the engine without scheduled replacement. Specific Honda V6 (J35) Odyssey, Pilot, and Acura MDX engines still use belts.

## Why it matters in Canada

For Canadian used-vehicle buyers, timing-belt service history is the most material due-diligence item on certain models. The Honda J35 V6 timing-belt service interval is 168,000 km or 8 years — and the Odyssey, Pilot, and Acura MDX use that engine. A 2018 Pilot at 175,000 km without timing-belt service history is carrying a $1,200 to $1,800 deferred maintenance liability and a real risk of catastrophic engine failure if the belt has stretched or worn. The same vehicle with verified service records showing belt replacement at 145,000 km is in completely different shape mechanically.

Other Japanese applications still using timing belts include certain older Subaru EJ-series engines, certain pre-2010 Mitsubishi V6 applications, and selected Toyota truck/SUV V6 engines. For any used Japanese vehicle from approximately 2010 backward, verify whether the engine uses a belt or chain, then verify service history accordingly. For modern (post-2015 typical) production, the engine almost certainly uses a chain.

## Common questions

### Does my Japanese car have a timing belt or timing chain?

Modern Japanese engines (post-2015 production typical) use timing chains in most applications. Specific exceptions: the Honda J-series V6 (3.5L) fitted to Odyssey, Pilot, Ridgeline, and Acura MDX uses a timing belt with a 168,000 km / 8-year service interval. Some older Toyota 2GR-FE V6 applications used belts before transitioning to chains. Many pre-2010 Honda four-cylinders, Subaru EJ-series boxers, and Mitsubishi V6 applications used belts. To confirm definitively for a specific vehicle, check the owner's manual, the manufacturer's online service guide, or call any same-brand dealer parts counter and provide the [VIN](/glossary/vin/).

### When should a timing belt be replaced on a Honda?

The Honda J-series V6 timing-belt interval is 168,000 km or 8 years, whichever comes first. This applies to the Odyssey, Pilot, Ridgeline, and Acura MDX with the 3.5L V6 engine. Service includes replacement of the timing belt itself plus the water pump (which is driven by the timing belt and almost universally replaced together), tensioner, and idler pulleys. The total service cost typically runs $1,200 to $1,800 CAD installed at a Honda or Acura dealer, slightly less at independent shops with Honda specialty experience. Skipping the service or extending past the interval risks catastrophic engine damage if the belt fails.

### What happens if a timing belt breaks?

On an interference engine — which includes the Honda J35 V6, the Toyota 2GR-FE V6, and most modern Japanese performance engines — a broken timing belt causes the open valves and the rising piston to meet at a force that bends the valves and often damages the cylinder head and pistons themselves. The repair typically requires either a complete engine rebuild ($4,000 to $7,000 CAD on a Honda V6) or replacement with a used or remanufactured engine ($3,500 to $6,000 plus labour). On the small number of non-interference engines (rare in modern Japanese production), the engine simply stops running and can be repaired by replacing only the broken belt — but most interference designs do not offer this best-case outcome.

### How much does timing belt service cost in Canada?

Honda J-series V6 timing-belt service typically runs $1,200 to $1,800 CAD installed at a Honda or Acura dealer, including the belt, water pump, tensioner, idler pulleys, and labour. Independent Japanese-car shops in Toronto, Vancouver, Calgary, and Montreal often perform the same service for $900 to $1,400 with [OEM](/glossary/oem/)-equivalent parts. For Subaru EJ-series boxer engines (older Outback, Forester, Legacy), the timing-belt service typically runs $900 to $1,400. Older Toyota and Mitsubishi V6 timing-belt service falls in the same general range. Modern Japanese engines using timing chains do not require this scheduled service.
