## What is OEM?

An OEM part — Original Equipment Manufacturer — is a replacement component manufactured to the exact specification of the part the vehicle came with from the factory. The same engineering tolerance, the same materials, the same supplier in many cases. When you order an OEM brake pad for a 2018 Toyota Camry, you are getting the part that left the Cambridge, Ontario assembly line on a new Camry the year it was built.

The line between OEM and aftermarket is not always clean. Many of the components Toyota, Honda, and Nissan ship from the factory are made by third-party suppliers — Denso, Aisin, NGK, KYB, Bridgestone — that also sell the same component into the aftermarket under their own brand. A Denso oxygen sensor sold through a Toyota dealer is OEM. The same Denso sensor sold through PartsAvatar.ca with a Denso label is technically aftermarket but functionally identical. The price difference can be 30 to 60 percent. This is one of the genuine value plays in the Canadian parts market for Japanese vehicles.

The category that does behave like marketing claims is the no-name aftermarket. Brake pads, oxygen sensors, and timing components from unknown manufacturers can vary widely in quality, and the cost saving versus an OEM-equivalent supplier brand is rarely worth the risk on safety-critical systems.

## Why it matters in Canada

Canadian dealer networks for the nine Japanese brands sold here — Toyota, Honda, Nissan, Mazda, Subaru, Lexus, Acura, Infiniti, Mitsubishi — honour OEM-part warranties when installed by a licensed shop, typically 12 months or 20,000 kilometres. That warranty travels with the part. If a dealer-installed OEM water pump fails at 18 months on a vehicle that has changed hands, the second owner can still claim against it, which is materially different from the warranty exposure on an aftermarket equivalent.

There is one Canada-specific gotcha for [JDM](/glossary/jdm/) imports. A vehicle imported from Japan under the 15-year rule may carry components with Japan-market part numbers that do not cross-reference cleanly to the Canadian-spec equivalent. The hardware is usually identical, but the parts catalogue at a Toyota Canada dealer is keyed to the [VIN](/glossary/vin/) of a Canadian-market vehicle. Buyers of imported JDM vehicles should expect to source some components either through a JDM specialist (Amayama, Megazip) or by walking the part number to the dealer parts counter for manual cross-reference.

## Common questions

### Are OEM parts always better than aftermarket parts?

For safety-critical components — brakes, suspension, airbag modules, anything in the steering or fuel system — OEM is the safer default. Quality control is tighter and the engineering tolerances are validated against the rest of the vehicle. For consumable items like cabin air filters, wiper blades, or interior trim, an OE-equivalent aftermarket part from a brand that supplies the original (Denso, NGK, Aisin, KYB) is usually a better value at 40 to 60 percent of the dealer price. The rule of thumb: if a failure could hurt someone, buy OEM; if it can only inconvenience you, an OE-equivalent aftermarket part from a known supplier is fine.

### Do OEM parts come with a warranty in Canada?

Yes. Most Canadian Japanese dealer networks warranty an OEM part installed by a licensed shop for 12 months or 20,000 kilometres, whichever comes first. The warranty covers the part and labour to replace it if the failure is attributable to the part itself rather than installation error or external damage. Aftermarket warranties exist but vary by brand and retailer — RockAuto, PartsAvatar, and PartsEngine Canada each have separate policies — and typically cover the part only, not labour.

### Where can I buy OEM parts for a Japanese car in Canada?

Direct from the manufacturer's Canadian parts portal — parts.toyota.ca, parts.honda.ca, parts.nissan.ca and the equivalent for the other six brands — or by walking into any same-brand dealer in your city. Most Canadian dealer parts counters will also order parts to the customer that they do not stock, typically with one to three day turnaround in the six Tier-1 metropolitan areas. For JDM-spec vehicles, Amayama and Megazip ship genuine OEM parts directly from Japan to Canadian addresses with typical 7 to 14 day delivery, plus CBSA clearance under the CUSMA agreement.

### Do OEM parts from Japan fit a Canadian-spec car?

Often yes, sometimes no. Mechanical components — engine internals, transmission internals, suspension hardware on a vehicle that was sold in both markets — are usually identical and the Japanese OEM part will fit perfectly. Body panels, lighting assemblies, electronics, and HVAC components are more likely to differ because Canadian-market vehicles must meet Canadian Motor Vehicle Safety Standards that diverge from Japanese standards. Always confirm cross-reference with a dealer parts counter using your vehicle's [VIN](/glossary/vin/) before ordering an OEM-Japan part for a Canadian-market vehicle.
