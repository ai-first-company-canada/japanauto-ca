## What is the VSA?

The Vehicle Sales Authority of British Columbia, abbreviated VSA, is the delegated administrative authority that regulates retail motor-vehicle dealers and salespersons in British Columbia. The VSA operates under the Motor Dealer Act and the Motor Dealer Customer Compensation Fund Regulation, and was established in 2004. Like Ontario's [OMVIC](/glossary/omvic/) and Alberta's [AMVIC](/glossary/amvic/), the VSA is a not-for-profit body operating at arm's length from the provincial government to license, supervise, and discipline the automotive retail industry.

The VSA's responsibilities include licensing every motor-dealer and salesperson in British Columbia, enforcing the Motor Dealer Act and its associated regulations, administering a mandatory salesperson certification course, and operating the BC Motor Dealer Customer Compensation Fund — an industry-funded compensation pool that reimburses consumers for verifiable losses caused by VSA-licensed dealers.

The licensing covers both new- and used-car sellers. A dealer selling used Japanese vehicles in Vancouver, Victoria, Surrey, Burnaby, or any other BC market must hold a current VSA licence, must display the licence number on advertising including online listings on japanauto.ca, and must comply with the Motor Dealer Act's disclosure obligations on every retail sale.

## Why it matters in Canada

Vancouver is a Tier-1 CMA on japanauto.ca and one of Canada's largest [JDM](/glossary/jdm/)-import hubs. Vehicles arriving from Japan via the Port of Vancouver land at Roberts Bank or Vanterm container terminals, then move into BC dealer networks or onto trucks for buyers across Western Canada. Every BC dealer that handles those imports must hold a current VSA licence, and the VSA disclosure rules apply to every retail sale.

The BC Motor Dealer Customer Compensation Fund is structurally similar to OMVIC's compensation fund and AMVIC's compensation fund. Each provincial fund operates independently and pays claims only against dealers licensed in that specific province. A BC consumer who suffered a loss from an Ontario dealer would need to claim against the OMVIC fund (Ontario), not the BC fund. Cross-border transactions between provinces add complexity and are one reason BC buyers are usually advised to purchase from VSA-licensed BC dealers rather than out-of-province sellers.

The VSA dealer search tool at mvsabc.com allows buyers to verify dealer licence status, registered business address, and disciplinary history before transacting.

## Common questions

### Is the VSA the same as OMVIC?

No. The VSA regulates British Columbia under the Motor Dealer Act. [OMVIC](/glossary/omvic/) regulates Ontario under the Motor Vehicle Dealers Act, 2002. [AMVIC](/glossary/amvic/) regulates Alberta under the Consumer Protection Act. Each provincial regulator operates independently, issues its own licences, runs its own compensation fund, and applies its own disclosure rules and fee schedules. The three bodies cooperate informally on cross-border issues but do not share licensing authority. A dealer licensed by the VSA has no automatic standing in Ontario or Alberta and must obtain separate provincial licences to operate there.

### How do I check a BC car dealer's licence?

Go to mvsabc.com and use the VSA dealer search tool. Enter the dealer's legal business name, trade name, or VSA licence number from the dealer's advertisement. The result page shows the licence status (active, expired, suspended, or revoked), expiry date, registered business address, and any public disciplinary actions taken in the previous several years. If the dealer cannot provide a licence number, or if the number does not match an active record, walk away. Every VSA-licensed dealer is required to display the licence number on advertisements including online listings on japanauto.ca.

### Does the BC Motor Dealer Compensation Fund cover private sales?

No. The BC Motor Dealer Customer Compensation Fund administered by the VSA reimburses consumers only for verifiable losses caused by VSA-licensed dealers. Private sales are outside the VSA's regulatory scope and outside the compensation fund's coverage — the Motor Dealer Act applies only to licensed dealers. Private buyers should rely on direct due diligence including a [CarFax Canada](/glossary/carfax/) report, a [Lien](/glossary/lien/) search through the BC Personal Property Registry, an ICBC Vehicle Claims History Report, and a pre-purchase inspection. The compensation fund and VSA disclosure rules do not apply to private transactions.

This information reflects regulations effective May 2026 and BC sources cited above. For binding advice on a specific transaction, consult a VSA-licensed advisor or BC's consumer protection office.
